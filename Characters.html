Characters

Jean Louise Finch
Known as "Scout", she lives with her father, her brother, and their black cook inside of Maycomb. She is a good person who has a great admiration for her father.
Jeremy Finch
Known as "Jem", he is the brother of Scout. He loves football and sports, and protects his younger sister throughout the novel. He is shown to be a kind hearted person.
Atticus Finch
Scout and Jem's father, he is a lawyer in Maycomb with lots of family. He is a widow and has a dry sense of humor, but cares for his family. He is one of the only people inside of Maycomb who believes in racial equality, and defends Tom Robinson.
Calpurnia
The Finches' black cook. She is a motherly figure to Scout and Jem, and she is respected inside of the Finch house. She takes care of the cleaning and cooking necesary inside the house.
Tom Robinson
A black field hand who was accused of rape. He is one of the novel's "mockingbirds", a symbol of innocence destroyed by evil.
Charles Baker "Dill" Harris
Jem and Scout's friend who visits every summer. He is fascinated with the fabled "Boo Radley" and investigates him along with Jem and Scout.
Arthur "Boo" Radley
A man who is fabled to be a monster, and never steps outside of his house. He is inside the imaginations of Scout, Jem and Dill. He is discovered to be a good hearted childlike person, who only wants to have friends, swathed in an initial shroud of creepiness. He was emotionally damaged by his father, but stayed strong and tried to reach out for help. He is one of the novel's "mockingbirds".
Bob Ewell
A drunk, mainly unemployed member of the Ewells, Maycomb's poorest and least respected family. He claimed that Tom Robinson had raped his daughter. He is represented as the secondary villian of this story.
Mayella Ewell
Bob Ewell's daughter. She is abused, lonely, and unhappy. She is pitied by many and wanted a friend.
Alexandria Finch
Atticus's sister, known as "Auntie Alexandria". She is a strong-willed woman stuck to her ways of being a "perfect sourthern lady". She wants Scout to be ladylike like her and her friends, but Scout mainly refuses and stays mostly a Tomboy.
Maudie Atkinson
Known as "Miss Maudie", she is the neighbor of the Finches. She is a sharp-tongued widow who is good friends with Jem and Scout. She also shares Atticus's passions for justice.
Henry Lafayette Dubose
An elderly woman known as "Mrs. Dubose" who is ill-tempered, racist, and speaks her mind often. She lives near the Finches and comments frequently on the way Scout looks and her not being Ladylike. She battles with a morphine addiction and Atticus gave Scout and Jem as punishment to spend their time reading books to Mrs. Dubose in her final moments.
